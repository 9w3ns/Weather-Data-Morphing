What is UTCI and Why Does It Matter?The Universal Thermal Climate Index (UTCI) is an internationally standardised biometeorological index that quantifies how the human body experiences outdoor thermal conditions. Unlike simpler temperature metrics, UTCI models the actual physiological response of the body — the combined effect of air temperature, radiation, wind, and humidity on heat exchange — producing a single equivalent temperature value calibrated to a reference person.UTCI was developed through the COST Action 730 programme (Europe, 2001–2007) and adopted by the World Meteorological Organization (WMO) as the preferred standard for outdoor human thermal comfort assessment. It is the basis for public heat-warning systems in Germany, Italy, the Netherlands, and increasingly across Southeast Asia.1.1  The UTCI Stress ScaleUTCI outputs a continuous temperature value (°C equivalent) mapped to ten stress categories. For this thesis, the relevant range is:Stress CategoryUTCI Range (°C)Physiological RiskThesis RelevanceNo thermal stress18 – 26°CNeutral / baselineModerate heat stress26 – 32°CDiscomfort beginsStrong heat stress32 – 38°CRisk for vulnerable groupsVery strong heat stress38 – 46°CSevere risk — current Bangkok peakEXTREME heat stress> 46°CLife-threatening without intervention — 2070 Bangkok peakTable 1 — UTCI stress categories and physiological risk. The 2070 Bangkok projection moves the entire distribution from 'Strong' into 'Very strong' and 'Extreme' categories.2.  Why UTCI Consumes More EPW Data Than Any Other Comfort IndexA key reason UTCI is selected as the primary metric for this thesis is its comprehensive use of EnergyPlus Weather (EPW) file data. The EPW format contains 35+ data fields per hour across 8,760 hours per year. Most comfort indices use only two or three fields. UTCI uses four primary physical inputs that collectively draw on the broadest set of EPW fields:Air temperature (Tair)Mean Radiant Temperature (MRT) — derived from solar radiation fields (GHR, DNR, DHR) and sky conditionsRelative humidity (RH)Wind speed (v)The table below compares which EPW fields each major comfort index actually incorporates. UTCI captures the compound interaction — a scenario where temperature, humidity, radiation, and wind all shift together — whereas a two-variable index (such as Heat Index) would miss the radiation and wind components entirely, systematically underestimating stress in Bangkok's high-radiation, high-humidity future climate.3.  The Specific Problem UTCI Reveals: The Floor ShiftThe central analytical finding of this thesis — the UTCI Floor Shift — could not have been identified using any simpler metric. The floor shift describes a condition where:The future minimum UTCI (38.2°C) exceeds the current average UTCI (37.6°C). No outdoor grid point at any time of day in 2070 falls below the 'Very strong heat stress' category. Bangkok's extreme solar radiation load is a primary driver of 2070 UTCI peaks. A dry-bulb-only analysis would produce a floor shift figure roughly 5–8°C lower, obscuring the severity.The stress category thresholds (Strong / Very strong / Extreme) are calibrated to human physiology, not arbitrary temperature bands. Limitations of UTCI and How This Thesis Addresses ThemUTCI is the primary metric but is not treated as a complete picture in isolation. Two acknowledged limitations and their methodological responses:5.1  UTCI assumes a standard reference person (walking, 0.9 clo)UTCI is calibrated to a 35-year-old person walking at 4 km/h in standard clothing. Outdoor laborers resting in the sun, elderly residents moving slowly, and young children have different metabolic rates and surface-area-to-mass ratios — meaning their actual thermal stress is higher than UTCI reports. This thesis treats UTCI as a conservative lower bound for vulnerable group exposure, not a precise per-person prediction.5.2  UTCI does not encode absolute physiological survivabilityUTCI communicates stress categories but does not directly state whether the body can cool itself at all. Wet-bulb temperature analysis (Methodology Phase 05) is included specifically to address this: when wet-bulb temperature exceeds approximately 35°C, evaporative cooling fails regardless of UTCI category. This is the true survivability threshold. UTCI maps the gradient; wet-bulb defines the floor below which no passive design can succeed. Together they bracket the design problem precisely.5.3  Supplementary metrics consideredThe following metrics were evaluated as alternatives or supplements:MetricEPW Inputs UsedOutdoor Calibrated?Climate Projection Ready?Thesis SuitabilityUTCI4 (Tair, MRT, RH, v)YesYesPrimary metric — recommendedPET4 (Tair, MRT, RH, v)YesYesValid alternative — less standard in AsiaPMV/PPD3 (Tair, MRT, RH)No (indoor)YesNot appropriate for outdoorWBGT3 (Tair, RH, Tglobe)PartialYesSupplementary — occupational health refHI2 (Tair, RH)LimitedYesToo simple — misses radiation & windTable 5 — Comfort metric comparison. UTCI and PET are the only metrics fully calibrated for outdoor, climate-projection contexts. PET is a valid alternative but is less frequently used in Asian urban heat research, making UTCI more appropriate for literature comparison.WBGT is referenced in Section 5.2 above as a supplementary framing device to connect the thesis to occupational health standards (ISO 7243, WHO guidance for outdoor laborers). It is not used as a design driver.6.  Why This Metric Choice Supports the Thesis ArgumentThe thesis position is that Bangkok's 2070 outdoor thermal condition crosses a threshold that fundamentally changes the spatial relationship between public life and buildings. That argument requires:A metric that captures compound heat exposure — not air temperature alone. UTCI does this.A metric that produces categorical severity levels tied to human physiology — not arbitrary thresholds. UTCI's stress categories (Strong / Very strong / Extreme) are those levels.A metric that can be used as a form-finding input in Grasshopper + Ladybug, compatible with parametric optimisation workflows. UTCI has direct Ladybug Tools implementation.A metric that works with morphed future EPW files, specifically the Normal Shift Pattern output from CURA-lab. UTCI takes the same EPW inputs regardless of whether the file is present-day or morphed — the methodology is temporally neutral.A metric whose output changes are legible to a non-specialist audience (advisors, juries, policymakers). Saying 'the entire city shifts from Strong to Very strong heat stress' is communicable. A raw wet-bulb number is not.No other single metric satisfies all five conditions simultaneously. This is the methodological justification for selecting UTCI as the primary performance evaluation index for this thesis.Document Classification: Methodology Note — Thesis Working DocumentFile reference: 65020505_UTCI_metric_rationale.docx     |     Atin Punyanitya     |     KMITL Architecture     |     June 2026

PurposeThis experiment tests how the depth of combined vertical and horizontal shading fins affects outdoor thermal comfort (UTCI) on a generic Bangkok block, under the projected 2070 SSP5-8.5 climate scenario. The goal was to find the point at which extending the fins further stops producing a meaningful reduction in UTCI — the diminishing-returns threshold for passive shading.2. Results3.1 Per-Iteration UTCI DistributionAcross the 323 test points per iteration, the highest recorded UTCI value across all iterations was 46.28°C, occurring at the west and south-west grid points — consistent with low afternoon sun angles from those directions. The raw per-point plot shows a repeating ripple pattern, which is an artefact of how Grasshopper orders the test points (alternating south grid / north grid columns) rather than a real spatial signal.Figure 1. UTCI per test point, all iterations overlaid. Peak 46.28°C at west/south-west.3.2 Average Temperature vs. Shading LengthThe site-wide average UTCI decreases as shading length increases, but the rate of improvement drops off sharply. Average temperature falls from about 41.96°C at 0.20 m of shading to about 41.41°C at 3.60 m — a total reduction of roughly 0.55°C across the entire 3.4 m range tested. Most of that gain occurs in the first 1.5–2.0 m; beyond that, each additional 20 cm buys only a fraction of a degree.Figure 2. Average UTCI per iteration, plotted against shading length (m).3.3 Min / Max StabilisationTracking minimum, maximum, and mean UTCI per iteration revealed a stabilisation pattern:Minimum UTCI locks at 41.07064°C from 0.60 m of shading onward and does not change through 3.60 m.Maximum UTCI locks at 44.18714°C from 1.80 m through 3.40 m, then drops slightly at 3.60 m.Mean UTCI continues to decrease across the full range, but in progressively smaller increments.Working logic applied: once an iteration produces no change (Δ = 0) across three consecutive data sets (min, max, mean), further shading extension at that depth was treated as not worth pursuing — since minimum values typically stop changing once shading extends past 1.00 m, and maximum reduction was prioritised because it represents the most extreme exposure point.3.4 Selected IterationsBased on the stabilisation pattern, three iterations were selected for detailed comparison: 1.40 m, 2.00 m, and 2.60 m of shading length.Figure 3. Summary of FindingsShading is effective but bounded: extending fins from 0.20 m to 3.60 m reduces average UTCI by only ~0.55°C total, with most of that gain captured in the first 1.5–2.0 m.Minimum UTCI (41.07°C) is fixed from 0.60 m of shading onward — no amount of additional fin depth lowers it further at this sun angle and geometry.Maximum UTCI plateaus near 44.19–44.27°C from 1.80 m onward, with only marginal reduction (0.08°C) between 2.00 m and 2.60 m.The 1.40 m / 2.00 m / 2.60 m comparison set was selected as the range where additional shading depth produces measurable but rapidly diminishing returns.An open question remains whether the observed stabilisation in min/max values reflects a true geometric limit (the fin already blocks the maximum possible solar vector at this sun angle) or is partly an artefact of the 2.50 m analysis grid being too coarse to register smaller incremental shifts. This should be checked by re-running a subset of iterations at a finer grid resolution (e.g. 1.25 m) to see whether the locked values move.
