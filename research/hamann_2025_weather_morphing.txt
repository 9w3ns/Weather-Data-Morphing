ADVANCED WEATHER DATA MORPHING FOR FUTURE CLIMATE-BASED BUILDING SIMULATION. A MODULAR PYTHON TOOL UTILIZING ENHANCED MORPHING ALGORITHMS FOR EPW FILE GENERATION Sophie Hamann", Angelos Chronis", Oana Taut", and Theodoros Galanos" "Infrared City, Vienna, Austria ABSTRACT Climate change poses significant challenges for building design and performance simulation, requiring accurate future weather data. This paper presents a Python-based tool for generating future weather files using state-of-the-art morphing techniques. The tool implements the Bounded Temperature Weighted Stretch algorithm for dry bulb temperature and solar radiation, while utilizing enhanced Belcher morphing methods for other climate variables. Testing with Vienna weather data under the SSP370 scenario for 2041-2060 revealed notable projected changes: a slight mean temperature decrease of $0.5^{\circ}C$ reduced precipitation (-35.4%) especially during summer months and decreased solar radiation (-6.3%). Validation identified limitations in maintaining consistency between solar radiation components that require further refinement. The tool incorporates CMIP6 climate projections and high-resolution historical data, enabling more accurate future weather simulations for building design. This research advances morphing methodologies by implementing recent algorithmic improvements and utilizing current climate scenarios, contributing to more resilient building design under changing climate conditions. One critical element is the selection of Global Climate Models (GCMs), which are instrumental in projecting future weather data. Many GCMs already incorporate future scenarios, commonly referred to as Representative Concentration Pathways (RCPs) or Shared Socioeconomic Pathways (SSPs). 3.1 Morphing Algorithm The morphing algorithm by Belcher et al. [2] is a method designed to generate future weather data for building simulations by combining observed present-day weather data with climate change projections from GCMs. The technique adjusts baseline weather variables through three main operations: shifts (absolute changes to mean values), stretches (percentage changes to variance), or a combination of both. The original morphing method specifically adjusts both the mean temperature and the average diurnal temperature range while preserving the difference between average daily maximum and minimum temperatures [2]. This ensures the relative daily temperature fluctuations remain consistent with projections. However, the method
Hamann, Chronis, Taut, and Galanos does not independently preserve the projected changes in daily maximum and minimum temperatures. As a result, the new maximum and minimum temperatures generated by the morphing process may not exactly match the specific projected values for these extremes. This discrepancy arises because the method collectively adjusts the mean and range rather than individually modifying the maximum and minimum components [2, 4]. In addressing the limitations of morphing, Eames et al. [4] enhanced the algorithm by introducing bounded morphing calculations which incorporate constraints to prevent unrealistic outcomes. These constraints enforce limits on the maximum and minimum values of the distribution, alongside adjustments to the mean values. Here, their purpose is to ensure that the weather data remains realistic and avoids implausible extremes. This approach is critical for creating reliable weather files for applications like building energy simulations and designing climate-resilient structures. 3.2 The Morphing Algorithm Applied At the present state, the developed python-based tool morphs six .epw variables - Dry Bulb Temperature, Dew Point Temperature, Precipitable Water, Relative Humidity, Wind Speed, and Global Horizontal Radiation. Dry Bulb Temperature and Global Horizontal Radiation are being morphed using the Bounded Weighted Stretch Morphing Algorithm as described in Eames et al. [4], as they specifically focus on these in their paper. For Dew Point Temperature, Precipitable Water, Relative Humidity and Wind Speed, the morphing procedure as described in Belcher et al. [2] is still suitable. More specifically, the formulas by Jentsch are being used to morph these variables. Jentsch is using the algorithm with slight improvements he made. The morphing formulas for these variables can be found in the Technical Reference manual [3]. 3.2.1 Dry Bulb Temperature Morphing There are three different types of changes that must be accounted for in temperature morphing: the change in mean daily temperatures $(\Delta T_{tas})$, the change in daily maximum temperature $(\Delta T_{tasmax})$, and the change in daily minimum temperature $(\Delta T_{tasmin})$. The normalization process maps the temperature values to a [0,1] range, which makes it easier to apply the morphing transformations. Eames uses the following formula to do so (1) $x=\frac{T-min(T)}{max(T)-min(T)}.$ Here, min(T) is the daily minimum temperature and $max(T)$ represents the maximum daily temperature. Normalization is important, because it creates a uniform scale where 0 represents the minimum temperature, I represents the maximum temperature and the values in between represent proportional distances from min to max. It allows the morphing transformations to be applied consistently. After applying the transformations, it can be "denormalized" back to actual temperatures using (2) $T^{\prime}=T_{min}^{\prime}+x^{\prime}(T_{max}^{\prime}-T_{min}^{\prime})$, (2)
Hamann, Chronis, Taut, and Galanos where $T_{min}^{\prime}$ is the future minimum temperature $T_{min}+\Delta T_{tasmin},$ $T_{max}$ is the future maximum temperature $T_{max}+\Delta T_{tasmax}$ and $x^{\prime}$ is the transformed normalized value. This approach ensures that the minimum temperature increases by $\Delta T_{Iasmax}$, the mean temperature increases by $\Delta T_{tas}$, and all the intermediate temperatures are transformed in a physically consistent way. This is a key improvement over simpler morphing methods that might just shift all temperatures by the same amount or scale them uniformly, as it preserves the complex relationships between minimum, maximum, and mean temperature changes. Then, a transfer function is applied. The transfer function serves as a "weighting curve" that determines how much transformation should be applied at each point in the normalized temperature range. $g=x^{m}(1-x)^{n}$ (3) Here, m and n are coefficients. The function (3) provides a maximum transformation in the middle of the temperature range, preserves the minimum $(x=0)$ and maximum $(x=1)$ temperatures and smoothly varies the amount of transformation between extremes. This function is critical as it preserves physical limits (minimum and maximum temperatures), provides smooth transitions between extremes and helps maintain the physical relationships between temperatures. Now, the scaling factor needs to be calculated (4). S $=(\frac{T_{mean}^{\prime}-T_{min}^{\prime}}{T_{max}^{\prime}-T_{min}^{\prime}})\times(\frac{max(T)-min(T)}{mean(T)-min(T)})-1$ (4) The first part of the equation represents where the mean should be in the future distribution. The second part represents where the mean is in the current distribution. The subtraction of I ensures that when these ratios are equal (no change needed), S becomes 0. Additionally, if $S>0$, the distribution is stretched, increasing the difference between extremes. If S $S<0.$ the distribution is compressed, reducing the difference between extremes. The scaling factor S is a critical component of the BTWS (Bounded Temperature Weighted Stretch) morphing method. Its main purpose is to ensure that the morphed temperatures preserve the change in mean temperature, the change in daily maximum temperature as well as the change in daily minimum temperature. In a final step, the transformation needs to be applied (5) $x^{\prime}=x+\frac{S\tilde{x}g}{\tilde{g}}$ (5) where $\overline{x}$ is the monthly average value of the variable. This equation is the core transformation, where x is the normalized temperature between 0 and 1, $x^{\prime}$ is the transformed normalized temperature, S is the scaling factor, g is the transfer function value for this temperature and are the mean transfer function values for the day. The scaling factor S adjusts how much the transfer function (g) affects the temperature transformation. When applied in the morphing equation, a larger S value results in more stretching of the temperature distribution and a smaller S value results in less stretching. 3.2.2 Solar Radiation Morphing The Solar Radiation Morphing corresponds to the Global Horizontal Radiation variable in the .epw file. Each Morpher modifies a specific climate variable. The advantage of this modular architecture is that morphers can be easily added. The internal working of the tool starts with the user selecting a target city, a SSP scenario and the wished time period. This occurs in main.py, where the script prompts the user to choose these parameters. After this, an instance of the EpwMorphing Manager is created to manage data organization, the morphing process as well as the file handling of the epw, .csv and .tif files. This initialization happens in main.py, where the EPWMorphingManager (base_path, epw_path and output_path) is called. The organize_files() function in the EPWMorphingManager then loads the historical climate data as well as the future climate projections and stores these datasets in self_organized.files in epw_morphing_manager.py. The script registers climate variable- specific morphers and the epw file is read, extracting the key climate data columns. Each morpher retrieves historical and future .tif datasets and uses bilinear interpolation to estimate the adjusted values. This is necessary, because the historical and future climate datasets have a resolution of 2.5 minutes, whilst the epw file has a resolution of 1 minute. The epw data is then modified accordingly. After the morphing process is complete, a new.epw file is created and the original values are being replaced by the modified ones. This ensures the future climate scenario is reflected in .epw format. Additionally, the epw file is converted into .csv format for easier analysis. In the end, comparison visualizations are created to show the original and morphed Dew Point Temperature, Dry Bulb Temperature, Relative Humidity, Wind Speed, Precipitation and Solar Radiation. 3.4 User Workflow For this experiment, 10 European cities were selected, namely Copenhagen, Frankfurt am Main, Barcelona, Geneva, Hamburg, London, Munich, Oslo, Stockholm, and Vienna. For these, the epw files, derived from TMYx weather data were loaded into the tool. The files were collected from the epw map supplied by Ladybug Tools [8]. Unlike the earlier RCP (Representative Concentration Pathways) scenarios, the newer SSP-based scenarios offer a more comprehensive framework by integrating economic and social factors for the projected emission trends and land use changes. Fick and Hijmans [11] created a dataset of spatially interpolated gridded climate data in .tif data format which is used for the tool discussed in this paper. The data can be downloaded at worldclim.org and is available at different spatial resolutions, the highest being at 30 seconds or approximately $1~km^{2}$. In the tool described here, the second highest resolution, 2.5 minutes of a degree of longitude and latitude, is being used. For this tool, WorldClim version 2.1 was downloaded and used in the calculations. [12] The data contains monthly average climate data for weather stations for 1970-2000. By taking satellite data and various co-variables, the data was interpolated "using the thin-plate smoothing spline algorithm [...] and [...] global climate surfaces for monthly precipitation and minimum mean, and maximum temperature, solar radiation, wind speed and vapour pressure [were created] (that is, 12 gridded surfaces per variable)" [11]. These monthly averages for temperature were determined by calculating the mean of the maximum and minimum temperatures recorded monthly. Additionally, data is also available for 19 bioclimatic variables which are frequently used in ecological analyses. 3.5.2 Data used for Future Projections Global Climate Models (GCMs) are complex computational tools that simulate the Earth's climate system through interactions between oceanic, atmospheric, and terrestrial components. These models process critical variables, including water vapour, carbon dioxide concentrations, heat transfer mechanisms, and planetary rotation, in order to project future climate conditions. GCMs are considered to be fundamental tools
Hamann, Chronis, Taut, and Galanos for both climate science and the development of international policy. Despite their sophistication, GCMs operate at coarse spatial resolutions, which limits their use for regional analysis. This limitation necessitates the development of downscaling techniques to translate broad-scale GCM outputs into higher-resolution regional projections. [13] The data used for future climate projections are downloaded at WorldClim. Here, data from CMIP6 is being downscaled and bias corrected with WorldClim version 2.1. Monthly values of minimum temperature, maximum temperature, and precipitation were processed. These climate projections were calculated for SSPs - SSP126, SSP245, SSP370, and SSP585. The monthly values represent averages over 20-year periods, specifically 2021-2040, 2041-2060, 2061-2080, and 2081-2100. These time intervals provide a long-term climate perspective, helping to analyze trends under different scenarios. [12] The GCM used for the change factor calculation within the discussed tool is called UKESM1-0-LL (full name: UKESM1.0-N96ORCA1) and has been released in 2018 as a fully coupled Earth System Model (ESM) developed by the UK Met Office Hadley Center as part of the sixth phase of the Coupled Model Intercomparison Project (CMIP) and is an important source for the Sixth Assessment Report of the IPCC (AR6) [14, 15, 16]. It integrates atmosphere, ocean, land, ice, and biogeochemistry components at high vertical resolution, making it one of the most sophisticated models for understanding climate change. UKESM1.0N96ORCA1 provides monthly temperature & precipitation projections averaged over 20-year periods. The GCM was selected as the future data for the developed tool and has been downscaled by WorldClim as described above. This is where the bounded morphing algorithm developed by Eames et al. comes into play. In conclusion, the key goal of morphing is to adjust historical weather data to reflect projected climate changes while preserving realistic weather variability. 4 RESULTS For a descriptive analysis, Vienna was chosen under the SSP370 climate scenario (2041-2060). Hence, careful thought has to be put into how to validate morphed .epw files. For this study, it was decided to use a combined approach of mathematical and comparative validation, using a tool called the Future Weather Generator. [18] 5.1 Validation for Solar Radiation Global Horizontal Irradiance (also known as Global Horizontal Radiation) is the total solar radiation received on a horizontal surface at ground level. Measured in watts per square meter $(W/m^{2})$, it combines two primary components: Direct Normal Irradiance and Diffuse Horizontal Irradiance. Global Horizontal Irradiance represents the sum of direct sunlight striking the surface and diffuse radiation from all parts of the sky. The mathematical relationship between these components is expressed as seen in (6) $GHI=DNI\cdot cos(\theta)+DHI.$ (6) In this context, GHI refers to Global Horizontal Irradiance, which is the total solar radiation received on a horizontal surface. DNI stands for Direct Normal Irradiance, representing the portion of solar radiation received directly from the Sun. DHI refers to Diffuse Horizontal Irradiance, which is the scattered radiation received from the sky dome. The symbol  denotes the solar zenith angle, which is the angle between the sun and the point directly overhead. This measurement is fundamental for solar energy applications, building design, and climate studies as it quantifies the total available solar resource at a given location. [19] For validation within this study, the consistency between Global Horizontal Irradiance (GHI), Diffuse Horizontal Irradiance (DHI), and Direct Normal Irradiance (DNI) is analyzed by ensuring that DHI < GHI (i.e., the diffuse component should not exceed the global irradiance), and by verifying that the calculated DNI, $DNI_{calc}=\frac{GH-DHI}{cos(\theta)},$ as seen in (7) is consistent with the provided DNI values within the morphed. epw file. In a first step within this validation methodology, the zenith angle  is being calculated which is being used in $cos(\theta)$ within (6) to determine the precise and always constant solar zenith angle for each time step. Next, the method checks whether diffuse radiation exceeds global radiation-which is physically impossible-and applies a $1~W/m^{2}$ tolerance to account for measurement or rounding errors. Then, the DNI consistency is being checked by recalculating DNI using (7), filtering for daytime hours and comparing calculated with provided DNI values using multiple metrics. Validation of the epw file generated using the python-based tool introduced in this paper reveals that the tool still exhibits certain limitations. For instance, 27.26% of the hours (2,388 hours) in the morphed file demonstrate instances where the diffuse horizontal irradiance (DHI) exceeds the global horizontal irradiance (GHI). This deviation from expected physical consistency indicates that diffuse horizontal radiation should not generally exceed total global horizontal radiation. Additionally, a negative mean difference of -188.55 $W/m^{2}$ between the recalculated and provided direct normal irradiance (DNI) suggests a notable discrepancy. The mean absolute percentage error of 292.81% further indicates some inconsistencies. These deviations are likely attributable to the fact that, while DNI and DHI remain unchanged in the morphed .epw file, GHI was reduced by 49.35%, thereby disrupting the typical relationship among these variables. This underscores the necessity for refinement of the morphing process to enhance internal consistency. These inconsistencies can be attributed to several factors. First, the calculation of solar geometry relies on hourly resolution, which may not always precisely capture the solar angle, especially during sunrise and sunset hours. Second, the morphing process itself does not fully account for dynamic atmospheric conditions such as cloud cover or aerosol variability, which can significantly affect irradiance values but are difficult to model accurately with a simplified approach. In addition, typical data challenges in high-latitude locations such as abrupt transitions during morning and evening hours can introduce further deviations in the radiation balance within .epw files. Minor errors in one variable may also propagate through derived variables, compounding the overall inconsistency. The spatial resolution of the climate data used also plays a role. In this study, the closest grid point was selected to represent the target location, a method that is fast and simple. While efficient, it does not involve interpolation between grid cells, which may result in reduced accuracy, particularly in regions with complex topography or near coastlines. Finally, this study uses climate projections from a single Global Climate Model (GCM). In most climate impact studies, it is standard practice to employ a GCM ensemble to account for the uncertainty and variability across different models. However, the scope of this paper is focused on the development of the morphing tool itself. Incorporating multiple GCMs, as well as refining the spatial and temporal resolution of inputs, is part of the planned future work and is discussed in the outlook section. 5.2 Validation of other Morphed Variables To enable validation of the other morphed variables, theepw file generated by the python-based tool introduced in this paper is compared against a corresponding.epw file created by the Future Weather Generator. Additionally, the tool uses the enhanced Belcher et al. [2] morphing algorithm, as refined by Jentsch [3], for Dew Point Temperature, Precipitation, Relative Humidity, and Wind Speed. Another goal was to use high-resolution data and gain a more comprehensive understanding of climate datasets and the process of morphing with relevance for future weather projections. In the next step, which is outside the scope of this research project, a more detailed and step-by-step way to check the morphed results should be created. Although an initial approach to verifying the results has already been used in this study, it needs to be improved to ensure that it is reliable and accurate. Errors in the morphed data may come from various sources, including the selection of Global Climate Model (GCM) data, the downscaling and bias correction applied by the researchers of WorldClim.org, and aspects of the morphing methodology itself-such as dataset selection and the handling of different data formats. In future work, the reliability of the projections could be improved by incorporating multiple Global Climate Models (GCMs) rather than relying on a single model. This would allow for a broader representation of climate variability and associated uncertainties. Using data from different climate models will make the results more robust. It will also be important to make sure that the results are all consistent with each other, especially when it comes to solar radiation variables like GHI, DNI, and DHI. Also, using a wider set of climatic variables will allow for a more comprehensive and realistic representation of future weather conditions. To better capture the range of possible future scenarios and support decision-making based on risk, we recommend integrating statistical percentiles (e.g., p5, p25, p50, p75, p95). These can provide valuable insights into uncertainty bands and support more detailed climate assessments, especially in applications such as building performance simulation and planning for how well cities can cope. Hamann, Chronis, Taut, and Galanos need for more innovation in simulating future weather conditions. A key goal of this work is that it aims to be transparent and can be reproduced. The tool is designed as an open and modular framework, making it adaptable for future extensions and allowing other researchers to build upon and validate the approach. It is very important that this kind of modelling can be repeated, because this makes sure that the results can be checked and made better as time goes on. Future .epw files are important for extreme weather events in building simulation to properly plan and construct buildings which will withstand these conditions in the future and to contribute to a resilient urban ecosystem and safe urban communities.
